My runners typically carry 3 pair of shoes to most meets assuming we don't already know the conditions. For fast grass fields, there are a ton of great XC spikes: something like Nike Dragonfly if money is not an issue or waffle it is. I am also a fan of Brooks Draft XC. Unfortunately, here in Texas there is nearly zero grass this time of year. Only had 1 meet all last year where spikes were appropriate. Using spikes assumes you have at least semi-decent form and are not a hard heel-striker. If you are, expect to have very sore legs after the run.

If the course requires running on concrete or sections of road, then go with a light-weight racing shoe (Saucony Endorphine, etc.). However, don't be afraid to go with a slightly heavier shoe with good stability and/or tread. Hard to go wrong with most of the dedicated distance running shoes from Saucony, Brooks, and Nike. If you like the Altras, then just get another pair. My experience is that runners have better times if they have more stability and can run more confidently. If they are worried about twisting an ankle or the clack-clack of spikes on concrete, they naturally slow down.

For challenging courses with rocks (VERY common here in Texas), a stability shoe is a must. In these conditions most runners used their daily training shoes (and a few used dedicated trail running shoes when conditions were really bad). Nothing worse than getting a stone bruise half-way through a 5K.

I have done some non-scientific comparisons with a couple of runners. Comparing the mid-level offerings from all the major brands, you will see almost no difference in times. Same shoe was faster on one person and slower on another just due to personal preferences and running style. Going to a super-light high-end shoe or spikes may be a few seconds faster but don't expect anything major (think potentially 5-10 seconds faster on a 5k). However, bulky/heavy shoes (i.e., trail shoes) will make you slower (seen 30 seconds slower vs a dedicated distance shoe).

In all cases, you want to wear the shoes 2 or 3 times before you race just to get used to them and make sure no-where rubs. You really don't want to wear them much more than that since most race shoes can't take a daily beating for a full season. You will also find that certain shoes require you to run slightly different due to how the plating reacts (Vaporflys are a good example).
